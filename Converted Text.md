The biological molecules that are produced by tumors or the bodys responses to tumors during tumorigenesis and progression, is known as Tumor biomarkers. Such biomarkers can be found in tumor tissues or the body fluids such as blood, urine, stool, as well as saliva, and they include genes, transcripts, proteins, and metabolites, all of which can give valuable insights for early diagnosis, prognosis prediction, recurrence detection, as well as effectively monitoring of cancers \[1], \[2]. In diseases such as cancer that are controlled by different genes, only studying the gain or loss of function of a certain protein encoded by a single gene to dissect the molecular complexity of the disease, is proven to be less effective. Hence, differential gene expression analysis, also known as expression genetics or functional genomics, has emerged as one of the most widely used strategies in discovering and understanding the molecular circuitry underlying cancer \[3].

Differential gene expression analysis is a strategy that determinates the numerous genes that respond to certain signals at specific conditions at once, by monitoring the downregulation or upregulation of such genes, creating a comprehensive depiction of the cellular functions \[4]. Upregulation of gene is when its expression is increased while, downregulation of genes is when its expression is reduced. In the context of cancer, upregulated genes might include those involved in cell proliferation, survival, or angiogenesis, which can contribute to tumor growth, while downregulated genes could be tumor suppressor genes that, when downregulated, allow uncontrolled cell growth \[5]. Identifying these genes can provide insights into the loss of regulatory mechanisms in disease progression and help in understanding the mechanisms driving the disease.

In recent years, bioinformatics has become a crucial factor in omics techniques, particularly in transcriptomics, DNA microarray, SAGE and RNA seq are widely used for gene expression profiling \[6], \[7]. In case of, RNA-sequencing (RNA-seq) techniques have given huge amounts of data on gene expression levels in multiple conditions at high resolution. This led to the necessity of the characterization of differentially expressed genes (DEGs) found in many medical samples and biological contexts, including disease identification, tumorigenesis, microbial studies, and evaluation of therapeutic efficacy in patients \[8], \[9].

In conclusion, differential expression analysis is a powerful way for identifying potential tumor biomarkers by comparing gene expression profiles between cancerous and non-cancerous tissues or between different stages of cancer. This approach can highlight genes whose altered expression is associated with tumorigenesis, progression, or response to therapy.
